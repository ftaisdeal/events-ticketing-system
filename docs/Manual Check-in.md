# Manual Check-in

In purely mechanical terms, a printed guest list with manual check-off is the *easiest* solution. It has the lowest technical complexity and the fewest points of failure.

However, it is only “easier” if your operational requirements stay below a certain threshold. Once you cross that threshold, manual check-in becomes harder *in practice* than either a PWA or a native app.

Below is a clear, operations-focused comparison.

#### **Where a printed list is genuinely the easiest**

A paper list works well when **all** of the following are true:

- **Small attendance** (dozens, maybe low hundreds)
- **Low arrival concurrency** (no rush window)
- **Simple access rules** (one ticket = one entry, no re-entry)
- **Low fraud risk** (trusted audience, no resale pressure)
- **No real-time reporting needed**

Advantages:

- Zero setup, zero training
- No devices, batteries, networks, or permissions
- Works in total connectivity failure
- Staff can operate it instantly

If you are running a community show, private event, or small seated performance, this is often the correct answer.

#### **Where manual check-in quickly becomes harder than software**

Manual systems fail *operationally*, not technically, under these conditions:

**1. Throughput and line pressure**

- Searching names alphabetically under pressure is slow.
- Handwriting and mark-off errors increase as stress rises.
- Parallel entry points are difficult to reconcile.

A single QR scan is ~0.5–1 second.
 A name lookup can easily be 5–15 seconds under load.

**2. Duplicate and fraud control**

Paper cannot reliably handle:

- Screenshot sharing
- Ticket resale
- Re-entry rules
- “Already admitted” detection across multiple doors

Once you add:

- Highlighting
- Notes
- Timestamps
- Multiple printed copies

…you’ve already re-created a fragile, manual database.

**3. Multi-door or staggered entry**

The moment you have:

- Two entrances
- VIP + GA
- Early access vs general access

…paper becomes coordination work, not check-in work.

**4. Post-event reconciliation**

With paper:

- Attendance counts are approximate
- Staff error is invisible
- Disputes (“I was turned away incorrectly”) are hard to resolve

Digital check-in gives you:

- Exact admission logs
- Time stamps
- Device/staff attribution

#### **The real comparison**

This is not **paper vs PWA vs app**.

It is:

**Static list** vs **shared source of truth**

Paper works when you don’t need a shared, real-time truth.

The moment you do, software—*even a simple one*—becomes easier overall.

#### **A pragmatic maturity ladder (what most successful teams do)**

1. **Paper list** – very small, trusted, low-risk events
2. **Single-device digital list** (tablet or phone) – still manual, but searchable
3. **QR scanning (PWA or app)** – when speed, scale, or fraud matter
4. **Dedicated native app + offline strategy** – high-stakes, high-volume operations

Skipping ahead too early adds cost.
 Staying too long on paper adds chaos.

#### **Direct answer to your question**

Yes, a printed list is easier **until it isn’t**.

If you expect:

- Lines,
- Multiple staff,
- Any ticket resale value,
- Or more than ~100–150 attendees arriving in a tight window,

…then QR-based check-in (PWA or app) will be *operationally easier* than paper, even though it is technically more complex.
